Gastroenterology

GI Conditions

Comprehensive clinical research on gastrointestinal conditions from BSG, ACG, EASL and NICE guidelines.

⚠️
All information is for educational research. Diagnosis requires clinical assessment by a qualified gastroenterologist. Do not self-manage significant GI symptoms without professional advice.

Quick Reference

GERD / Acid Reflux

  • Heartburn + regurgitation: cardinal symptoms
  • Alarm features (dysphagia, weight loss, anaemia): urgent endoscopy
  • PPIs (omeprazole 20mg OD) 30 min before breakfast: first-line
  • Barrett's oesophagus: requires endoscopic surveillance every 2-5 years

Peptic Ulcer / H. pylori

  • H. pylori eradication: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin x 7-14 days
  • Test-of-cure: urea breath test 4 weeks after antibiotics, 2 weeks off PPI
  • Gastric ulcers: biopsy mandatory + repeat scope to confirm healing
  • Stop NSAIDs if possible; long-term PPI if NSAIDs continued

IBD (UC / Crohn's)

  • Faecal calprotectin: best non-invasive IBD screening test
  • Severe UC (6+ bloody stools/day, systemic): HOSPITAL ADMISSION
  • Toxic megacolon (colon over 6cm): MEDICAL/SURGICAL EMERGENCY
  • Anti-TNF biologics after inadequate response to conventional therapy

IBS

  • Rome IV criteria: abdominal pain weekly x 3 months, linked to bowel habit
  • Low-FODMAP diet: 70% response in IBS-D, supervised by dietitian
  • Exclude red flags before diagnosing: age over 50, rectal bleeding, weight loss
  • CBT/gut-directed hypnotherapy: strong evidence for refractory IBS

GI Medicines

Comprehensive research on gastroenterology medications from BSG, ACG, EASL and NICE guidelines.

⚠️
Medication information is for research only. Never start, stop, or adjust GI medications without your gastroenterologist's guidance.

Acid Suppressants and Anti-Reflux Medications

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) — First-Line

  • Omeprazole 20-40mg OD: most widely used. Take 30 minutes before breakfast. Doses of 40mg for severe oesophagitis or H. pylori eradication.
  • Lansoprazole 15-30mg OD, Esomeprazole 20-40mg OD, Pantoprazole 20-40mg OD, Rabeprazole 10-20mg OD: alternatives, broadly equivalent efficacy. Some differences in CYP2C19 metabolism affecting drug interactions.
  • Duration: 4-8 weeks for oesophagitis/peptic ulcer. Long-term use in Barrett's oesophagus, severe GERD. Side effects with long-term use: hypomagnesaemia (check Mg if on long-term PPIs), Clostridium difficile risk, osteoporosis (modest), hyponatraemia.
  • IV omeprazole/pantoprazole: 80mg bolus then 8mg/hour infusion for high-risk peptic ulcer bleeding post-endoscopy (NICE guidance).

H2 Receptor Antagonists

  • Famotidine 20-40mg BD: ranitidine now largely withdrawn (NDMA contamination). H2 blockers less effective than PPIs. Useful as add-on for breakthrough nocturnal symptoms (taken at bedtime in addition to PPI).

Antacids and Alginate

  • Gaviscon (alginate) + antacid: forms raft to prevent reflux. Useful for symptomatic relief, pregnancy (safe), mild GERD. Not disease-modifying.
  • Magnesium/aluminium antacids: rapid symptom relief, not for regular use (diarrhoea/constipation).

H. pylori Eradication Regimens

  • First-line (UK/India): PPI (standard dose BD) + Clarithromycin 500mg BD + Amoxicillin 1g BD for 7-14 days. Metronidazole 400mg BD if penicillin allergy.
  • Second-line (clarithromycin failure): Bismuth quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole) or levofloxacin-based triple therapy.
  • Test-of-cure: Urea breath test (UBT) at least 4 weeks after antibiotics AND 2 weeks after stopping PPI. Stool antigen test equally valid.

IBD Medications

  • Aminosalicylates (5-ASA) — Mesalazine (Mesalamine): First-line for mild-moderate UC. Oral (Pentasa, Asacol) and/or rectal (suppositories for proctitis, enemas for left-sided UC). Minimal role in Crohn's disease.
  • Corticosteroids (prednisolone oral, IV hydrocortisone): Induction of acute flares only. Prednisolone 40mg OD reducing course over 8 weeks. NOT maintenance therapy. Budesonide (Budenofalk/Entocort): locally acting, less systemic absorption, used for mild-moderate Crohn's (ileocolonic disease) and microscopic colitis.
  • Thiopurines (Azathioprine 2-2.5mg/kg/day, Mercaptopurine): Maintenance therapy for UC and Crohn's. Check TPMT enzyme activity before starting (risk of severe myelosuppression in TPMT-deficient patients). Takes 3-4 months to achieve full effect. Regular FBC and LFTs monitoring. Risk of lymphoma (small but real), non-melanoma skin cancer (sun protection advice).
  • Methotrexate: Crohn's disease maintenance, particularly in thiopurine intolerance. 25mg SC weekly, reduce to 15mg after 16 weeks. Folic acid supplement. LFTs and FBC monitoring. Teratogenic — effective contraception essential.
  • Ciclosporin (IV): Rescue therapy for acute severe UC not responding to 3-5 days IV steroids. Highly effective short-term (60-80% avoiding colectomy). Bridge to thiopurine maintenance.

Liver and Biliary Medications

  • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) 13-15mg/kg/day: First-line for Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC). Slows disease progression, improves LFTs. Second-line add-on: obeticholic acid (OCA) or fibrates for inadequate UDCA response.
  • Spironolactone 100-400mg/day +/- Furosemide 40-160mg/day: First-line diuretic therapy for cirrhotic ascites. Target weight loss 0.5-1kg/day. Monitor renal function and electrolytes closely.
  • Terlipressin: Vasoconstrictor for acute variceal haemorrhage (with banding/sclerotherapy) and hepatorenal syndrome (with albumin infusion). IV administration.
  • Non-selective beta-blockers (Propranolol 20-40mg BD, Carvedilol 6.25mg BD): Primary and secondary prophylaxis of variceal bleeding in cirrhosis with varices. Reduce portal pressure. Titrate to heart rate 55-60 bpm. Caution in SBP and renal impairment.
  • Lactulose 15-30ml BD-TDS (titrated to 2-3 soft stools/day): First-line for hepatic encephalopathy. Acidifies colon, reduces ammonia absorption. Add rifaximin 550mg BD for recurrent episodes (NICE approved).
  • Albumin infusion: IV human albumin 20%. Used in: SBP (1.5g/kg at diagnosis, 1g/kg at 48 hours - reduces hepatorenal syndrome), large-volume paracentesis (6-8g per litre removed over 5 litres), hepatorenal syndrome (with terlipressin).
  • Norfloxacin 400mg OD / Co-trimoxazole: Long-term antibiotic prophylaxis for SBP in high-risk cirrhotic patients (prior SBP, low ascitic protein).
  • N-acetylcysteine (NAC): IV infusion for paracetamol overdose causing acute liver failure. Most effective within 8-10 hours but beneficial up to 24+ hours. Also used in non-paracetamol ALF.
  • Resmetirom (Rezdiffra): First FDA-approved treatment for MASH (metabolic-associated steatohepatitis) with liver fibrosis. THR-beta agonist. Reduces liver fat and fibrosis.

Bowel and Motility Agents

  • Loperamide 2-4mg (max 16mg/day): First-line for IBS-D and functional diarrhoea. Reduces intestinal motility and fluid secretion. Not for use in infectious diarrhoea or IBD active colitis.
  • Mebeverine 135-200mg TDS (antispasmodic): IBS abdominal cramps. Smooth muscle relaxant. Well tolerated.
  • Peppermint oil capsules (Colpermin): IBS abdominal pain and bloating. Good safety profile. Take before meals.
  • Linaclotide 290mcg OD (30 mins before breakfast): NICE-approved for moderate-severe IBS-C. Guanylate cyclase-C agonist — increases intestinal fluid and transit. Also licensed for chronic idiopathic constipation.
  • Ispaghula husk (Fybogel): Bulk-forming laxative for IBS-C and constipation. Take with adequate fluid. Caution in bowel obstruction. First-line fibre supplement.
  • Macrogol (Movicol/Laxido): Osmotic laxative. First-line for constipation, also bowel prep. Safe in pregnancy. 1-3 sachets daily.
  • Rifaximin 550mg BD x 14 days: Non-absorbable antibiotic for IBS-D (targets small bowel bacterial dysbiosis). Also 550mg BD long-term for hepatic encephalopathy prevention.
  • Amitriptyline 10-30mg at night: Low-dose TCA for IBS — modulates gut-brain axis. Good evidence for pain and diarrhoea in IBS. NICE recommended.

GI Biologics and Advanced Therapies

  • Anti-TNF agents — Infliximab (Remicade, Inflectra) IV infusion: Moderate-severe Crohn's and UC refractory to conventional therapy. Induction at 0, 2, 6 weeks then 8-weekly maintenance. Screen for TB, hepatitis B before starting. Risk of infections, reactivation of TB.
  • Adalimumab (Humira) SC injection: Self-administered anti-TNF for Crohn's and UC. 160mg at week 0, 80mg at week 2, then 40mg every 2 weeks.
  • Vedolizumab (Entyvio) IV/SC: Gut-selective anti-integrin (blocks alpha4beta7). Moderate-severe UC and Crohn's. Excellent safety profile (gut-selective, minimal systemic immunosuppression). Preferred in patients with history of malignancy or serious infections.
  • Ustekinumab (Stelara) IV/SC: Anti-IL12/23. Moderate-severe Crohn's (after anti-TNF failure or intolerance). Also licensed for UC. IV induction dose then 90mg SC every 8-12 weeks.
  • JAK inhibitors (Tofacitinib 10mg BD induction then 5mg BD, Filgotinib 200mg OD, Upadacitinib 45mg OD induction then 15/30mg OD): Oral targeted synthetic DMARDs for moderate-severe UC. Rapid onset. VTE risk (tofacitinib): caution in patients over 65/cardiovascular risk. MHRA guidance on prescribing.
  • Risankizumab (Skyrizi) IV/SC: Anti-IL23p19. Licensed for moderate-severe Crohn's and UC. Once-daily oral ozanimod (S1P modulator) also available for UC.

Antiviral Medications for Hepatitis B and C

Hepatitis B (HBV)

  • Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 245mg OD: First-line antiviral for Hepatitis B. Highly effective, well tolerated. Monitor renal function and bone density with long-term use. Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF) 25mg OD: better renal and bone safety profile, preferred in those with renal disease.
  • Entecavir 0.5mg OD (1mg OD in treatment-experienced): Alternative first-line antiviral. High barrier to resistance. Not if lamivudine-resistant.
  • Treatment may be lifelong. Aim: sustained viral suppression (HBV DNA undetectable), ALT normalisation, HBsAg loss (rarely achieved but possible with long-term treatment). HCC surveillance 6-monthly USS in all HBsAg+ patients with cirrhosis (and high-risk without cirrhosis).

Hepatitis C (HCV) — Direct-Acting Antivirals (DAAs)

  • Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (Epclusa) 1 tablet OD x 12 weeks: Pan-genotypic regimen, first-line for most patients. Over 95% sustained virological response (SVR = cure) across all genotypes.
  • Sofosbuvir/Ledipasvir (Harvoni) 1 tablet OD x 8-12 weeks: Genotype 1 and 4. 8 weeks if treatment-naive, non-cirrhotic, low viral load.
  • Glecaprevir/Pibrentasvir (Maviret) 3 tablets OD x 8 weeks: Pan-genotypic, 8-week treatment, minimal drug interactions, can be used in compensated cirrhosis and severe renal impairment.
  • HCV treatment cures the infection. Liver fibrosis regresses in many patients. HCC risk remains in those with cirrhosis — continue surveillance post-SVR if cirrhotic.
Endoscopy & GI Procedures

Endoscopic & GI Procedures

Research on endoscopic procedures, interventions and diagnostic techniques from BSG and ACG guidelines.

OGD (Oesophago-Gastro-Duodenoscopy) / Gastroscopy

Flexible endoscope passed through mouth to examine oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum. Performed under sedation (IV midazolam +/- fentanyl) or throat spray. Takes 5-15 minutes. Day-case procedure.

Indications

  • Dyspepsia with alarm features (dysphagia, weight loss, anaemia, haematemesis, age over 55 with new symptoms)
  • Upper GI bleeding: haematemesis or melaena
  • Barrett's oesophagus surveillance
  • Coeliac disease diagnosis (duodenal biopsies)
  • H. pylori biopsy (CLO test) and biopsies of gastric ulcers (to exclude malignancy)
  • Dysphagia assessment, oesophageal stricture dilation
  • Gastric cancer screening in high-risk populations

Common Findings

  • Oesophagitis: Los Angeles (LA) classification A-D (A=mild erosions to D=extensive ulceration)
  • Barrett's oesophagus: Prague classification (C and M measurements)
  • Gastric ulcer: location, Forrest classification of bleeding stigmata (Ia-IIc)
  • H. pylori gastritis pattern (antral predominant vs pangastritis)
  • Duodenal ulcer: typically posterior wall D1
  • Normal OGD: does not exclude GERD (functional endoscopy-negative reflux disease)

Colonoscopy

Gold-standard investigation of the large bowel (colon and rectum). Requires bowel preparation the day before (Moviprep, Klean-Prep, Citrafleet). Performed under sedation. Takes 20-45 minutes. Allows biopsy, polypectomy and other interventions.

Indications

  • Rectal bleeding or change in bowel habit over 40-50 years
  • IBD diagnosis, extent assessment, and surveillance for dysplasia
  • Colorectal cancer screening and post-polypectomy surveillance
  • Iron deficiency anaemia (with OGD)
  • Family history of colorectal cancer/polyps
  • Positive FIT (faecal immunochemical test)

Polyp Classification

  • Paris classification of polyp morphology: Ip (pedunculated), Is (sessile), IIa (flat elevated), IIb (flat), IIc (flat depressed)
  • Histology: hyperplastic (low risk), tubular adenoma, tubulovillous adenoma, villous adenoma (higher risk), serrated adenoma (flat, right-sided, higher malignant potential)
  • BSG post-polypectomy surveillance: low risk (1-2 adenomas, under 10mm, tubular, LGD): 3-year repeat. High risk (3+ adenomas, any over 10mm, villous, HGD): 1-year repeat.

ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography)

Specialised procedure combining endoscopy and fluoroscopy to diagnose and treat biliary and pancreatic duct conditions. Performed under general anaesthesia or deep sedation. Highest complication rate of all GI endoscopic procedures.

Indications

  • Common bile duct (CBD) stones (choledocholithiasis): stone extraction with sphincterotomy
  • Acute cholangitis: biliary decompression (stent or naso-biliary drain)
  • Malignant biliary obstruction (pancreatic cancer, cholangiocarcinoma): stent placement
  • Post-cholecystectomy bile leak: stent placement
  • Pancreatic duct strictures: stent placement in chronic pancreatitis
  • PSC: dominant stricture dilation

Complications

  • Post-ERCP pancreatitis (PEP): 3-5%. Risk reduced with PR diclofenac (given routinely) and pancreatic stent in high-risk cases
  • Cholangitis, haemorrhage (post-sphincterotomy), perforation: all rare but serious
  • Overall serious complication rate: 5-10%

Therapeutic Endoscopy

  • Endoscopic polypectomy: Snare polypectomy (pedunculated/large sessile), cold snare (small polyps under 10mm), EMR (endoscopic mucosal resection) for large flat lesions, ESD (endoscopic submucosal dissection) for en-bloc resection of superficial cancers (specialist centres).
  • Variceal band ligation (VBL): Elastic rubber bands applied to oesophageal varices. Primary treatment for acute variceal bleeding. Also primary and secondary prophylaxis in cirrhotics with high-risk varices (red wale marks, large size). Repeated every 2-4 weeks until variceal eradication.
  • Injection therapy: Adrenaline (1:10,000) injection around bleeding peptic ulcer vessel + thermal or clip haemostasis. Haemospraying (Hemospray) for diffuse bleeding.
  • Endoscopic clips (haemoclips): Applied to bleeding vessels, Dieulafoy lesions, perforations (endoscopic closure of small perforations).
  • Radiofrequency ablation (RFA): For Barrett's oesophagus with dysplasia. Delivers controlled thermal energy to ablate dysplastic mucosa. Combined with EMR of visible lesions.
  • Argon plasma coagulation (APC): Non-contact thermal therapy for bleeding angiodysplasia, radiation proctitis, gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE / watermelon stomach).
  • Oesophageal dilation: For peptic strictures (Savary-Gilliard bougies or balloon dilation), Schatzki rings, anastomotic strictures, post-RFA strictures.
  • PEG (Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy): Long-term enteral feeding access for patients unable to swallow (stroke, MND, head and neck cancer).

Capsule Endoscopy and Other Modalities

  • Capsule endoscopy (video capsule/pill camera): Patient swallows a small camera capsule (11x26mm) which photographs the small bowel as it passes naturally. Examines 5-7m of small intestine not accessible to standard endoscopy. Indicated for obscure GI bleeding (iron deficiency anaemia with negative bidirectional endoscopy), suspected Crohn's of small bowel, surveillance in hereditary polyposis syndromes (FAP, Peutz-Jeghers). CONTRAINDICATED in known or suspected bowel obstruction (retention risk).
  • Device-assisted enteroscopy (balloon enteroscopy): Single or double balloon enteroscopy allows diagnostic and therapeutic access to deep small bowel. Indicated when capsule endoscopy identifies a lesion requiring biopsy or treatment (polypectomy, haemostasis).
  • Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS): Endoscope with ultrasound probe at tip. Allows high-resolution imaging of GI wall layers, adjacent structures, lymph nodes. Indications: staging oesophageal/gastric/pancreatic cancer, assessing submucosal lesions, EUS-guided FNA of lymph nodes/pancreatic masses, drainage of pancreatic pseudocysts/walled-off necrosis.
  • Oesophageal manometry: Measures oesophageal motor function and lower oesophageal sphincter pressure. Essential before anti-reflux surgery (Nissen fundoplication) to exclude oesophageal dysmotility. Diagnostic for achalasia (absent peristalsis + failed LOS relaxation on HRM).

GI Emergency Guide

🚨
GI EMERGENCIES — CALL 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US) IMMEDIATELY: Vomiting blood, black tarry stools, sudden severe abdominal pain, rigid abdomen (perforation), jaundice with fever and rigors (cholangitis), signs of acute liver failure, toxic megacolon. These are time-critical life-threatening emergencies.

🚨 Upper GI Bleeding

Recognition: Haematemesis (vomiting fresh red blood or coffee-ground material) or Melaena (black tarry foul-smelling stools = digested blood from upper GI source).

Immediate Actions

  • Call 999/112/911 immediately for large bleeds or haemodynamic instability
  • Large bore IV access x2, fluid resuscitation (target systolic over 90mmHg)
  • Urgent blood tests: FBC, clotting, U&E, LFTs, group & save/crossmatch
  • Blood transfusion if Hb under 70-80 g/L (threshold 100 g/L if cardiovascular disease)
  • IV PPI (omeprazole 80mg bolus) for suspected peptic ulcer bleeding
  • Terlipressin IV if variceal bleeding suspected (cirrhotic patient)
  • Urgent endoscopy within 24 hours (12 hours if haemodynamically unstable or suspected varices)

🚨 GI Perforation

Recognition: Sudden onset severe generalised abdominal pain, board-like rigid abdomen, rebound tenderness, absent bowel sounds, haemodynamic instability. Pain may radiate to shoulder tip (diaphragmatic irritation from free gas).

Critical Points

  • SURGICAL EMERGENCY — call emergency services immediately
  • Most common causes: perforated peptic ulcer, perforated diverticulitis, colonic cancer perforation
  • Erect chest X-ray: free air under diaphragm (present in 70-80% of perforations)
  • CT abdomen and pelvis: confirms diagnosis, identifies source
  • IV antibiotics (broad-spectrum), IV fluids, nasogastric tube, urinary catheter
  • Emergency surgery (laparoscopic or open) or conservative management in selected cases

🚨 Acute Cholangitis

Charcot's Triad: Fever + Jaundice + Right upper quadrant pain. Reynold's Pentad (severe): adds Hypotension + Altered consciousness.

Critical Points

  • Caused by biliary obstruction (gallstones, strictures, cancer) + bacterial infection
  • Tokyo Guidelines severity grading: Grade I (mild), II (moderate), III (severe with organ dysfunction)
  • IV antibiotics immediately (piperacillin-tazobactam or co-amoxiclav + metronidazole)
  • Urgent ERCP for biliary decompression within 24-48 hours (within 12 hours for Grade III)
  • Blood cultures before antibiotics if possible

🚨 Acute Liver Failure

Recognition: Rapid onset jaundice + coagulopathy (INR over 1.5) + hepatic encephalopathy in previously normal liver, developing within 26 weeks.

Critical Points

  • Causes: paracetamol overdose (most common UK/India), viral hepatitis (HAV, HBV), autoimmune, Wilson's disease, Budd-Chiari, drugs
  • MEDICAL EMERGENCY: ICU level care required
  • N-acetylcysteine IV immediately for paracetamol overdose (effective even if late)
  • Contact liver transplant centre early (King's College criteria guide listing)
  • Manage hypoglycaemia, coagulopathy, encephalopathy, renal failure
  • Antiviral therapy if viral cause (entecavir for HBV, antivirals for HSV)

Other GI Emergencies

  • Toxic megacolon: Colonic dilatation (transverse colon over 6cm) + systemic toxicity in severe colitis (UC or C. difficile). ICU, IV steroids + antibiotics, surgical consultation. Emergency colectomy if no improvement in 24-72 hours.
  • Acute severe pancreatitis: Predicted severe (Ranson/Glasgow/CTSI criteria), ICU care, aggressive IV fluid resuscitation, early enteral nutrition, ERCP if CBD stones + cholangitis within 72 hours, surgical/radiological drainage for infected necrosis.
  • Bowel obstruction: Abdominal distension + colicky pain + vomiting + absolute constipation. Small bowel obstruction (most commonly adhesions): drip-and-suck. Large bowel obstruction (cancer, volvulus): CT urgently, endoscopic decompression, surgery. Volvulus: urgent endoscopic decompression if non-ischaemic.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: Severe abdominal pain out of proportion to examination findings. Causes: arterial (embolic/thrombotic), venous thrombosis, non-occlusive. CT angiography. Surgical emergency (bowel resection if necrosed).

Low-FODMAP Diet for IBS

FODMAP = Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides And Polyols. These poorly absorbed short-chain carbohydrates are fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas and drawing water into the bowel — triggering IBS symptoms. The low-FODMAP diet reduces these, with evidence of 70% response rate in IBS-D. Supervised by a dietitian is essential for correct implementation and avoiding nutritional deficiencies.

Phase 1 (2-6 weeks): Restriction Phase — Foods to Avoid

  • Oligosaccharides (fructans/GOS): Wheat, rye, onion, garlic, leek, spring onion (white part), beans, lentils, chickpeas, cashews, pistachios
  • Disaccharides (lactose): Cow's milk, soft cheeses (ricotta, cottage), ice cream, yoghurt
  • Monosaccharides (excess fructose): Apple, pear, mango, watermelon, honey, high-fructose corn syrup
  • Polyols: Avocado, blackberries, cauliflower, mushrooms, sorbitol/mannitol/xylitol/maltitol (sugar-free sweets, gum)

Low-FODMAP Foods Allowed

  • Grains: rice, quinoa, oats, polenta, gluten-free bread (note: wheat exclusion in low-FODMAP is for fructan content, not gluten)
  • Protein: eggs, tofu (firm), meat, fish, seafood
  • Dairy: lactose-free milk, hard cheeses (cheddar, parmesan), butter
  • Vegetables: carrot, cucumber, aubergine, green beans, potato, spinach, tomato, courgette, pepper
  • Fruit: banana, blueberry, grape, kiwi, orange, strawberry, pineapple

Phase 2: Reintroduction (6-8 weeks)

Systematic reintroduction of FODMAP groups one at a time to identify individual triggers. Most patients are sensitive to only 1-2 FODMAP groups, not all. Phase 3: long-term personalised diet based on identified triggers.

Gluten-Free Diet (Coeliac Disease)

Strict lifelong gluten-free diet is the only treatment for coeliac disease. Gluten = protein in wheat, barley, and rye. Oats are safe for most coeliac patients but must be certified gluten-free oats (risk of cross-contamination in regular oats). Even small amounts of gluten cause mucosal damage.

Foods Containing Gluten (Avoid Strictly)

  • Wheat: bread, pasta, couscous, semolina, spelt, kamut, durum wheat
  • Barley: beer, malt extract, barley water, some soups
  • Rye: rye bread, crispbreads
  • Hidden sources: sauces, gravies, soups, sausages, processed meats, stock cubes, medications (check excipients)

Naturally Gluten-Free Staples

  • Rice, corn/maize, potato, quinoa, buckwheat, millet, sorghum, tapioca
  • In India: most dals, rice-based preparations (idli, dosa, poha) are naturally gluten-free. Beware atta/maida in rotis and parathas. Check all packaged foods.
  • Gluten-free labelled products: must contain under 20ppm gluten (EU/UK standard)

Nutritional Supplementation

  • Iron: common deficiency in coeliac. Correct after starting GFD (improved absorption).
  • Folate (vitamin B9): often deficient in active coeliac disease
  • Calcium and Vitamin D: bone density affected. DEXA scan recommended.
  • Vitamin B12: less commonly deficient than in Crohn's but monitor

GERD and Acid Reflux Diet

Foods and Habits That Worsen Reflux

  • High-fat foods: delay gastric emptying, relax lower oesophageal sphincter
  • Coffee and caffeinated drinks: relax LOS, increase acid secretion
  • Alcohol: relaxes LOS, increases gastric acid, directly irritates oesophageal mucosa
  • Chocolate: contains methylxanthines that relax LOS
  • Citrus fruits and tomatoes: highly acidic, worsen oesophageal symptoms (though do not cause reflux)
  • Peppermint: relaxes LOS (note: peppermint oil capsules for IBS should be enteric-coated to bypass LOS)
  • Carbonated drinks: increase gastric pressure and gas
  • Large meal volumes: increases gastric pressure
  • Eating within 2-3 hours of lying down: supine position facilitates reflux

Lifestyle Measures

  • Elevation of head of bed 15-20cm (wedge pillow or bed blocks under head posts) — most evidence-based lifestyle intervention
  • Weight loss: reduces intra-abdominal pressure and reflux episodes significantly
  • Smoking cessation: smoking reduces LOS pressure and increases acid
  • Smaller, more frequent meals rather than large meals
  • Left lateral decubitus sleeping position reduces nocturnal reflux

Liver Disease and Cirrhosis Diet

General Principles

  • Protein: Adequate protein (1.0-1.5g/kg/day) is essential in cirrhosis. Old advice to restrict protein to prevent hepatic encephalopathy is WRONG and harmful — leads to muscle wasting (sarcopenia) which worsens survival. Late evening snack (50g complex carbohydrate): proven to reduce protein catabolism overnight.
  • Salt/Sodium restriction: Under 90mmol/day (under 2g sodium = under 5g salt/day) for patients with ascites. Strict no-added-salt diet. Avoid processed foods, ready meals, tinned soups, salty snacks, pickles.
  • Fluid restriction: Only needed if dilutional hyponatraemia (sodium under 125-130). Routine fluid restriction is NOT recommended for most patients with ascites.
  • Alcohol abstinence: Absolute. Even small amounts accelerate liver damage in alcoholic liver disease. Abstinence is the single most important intervention.
  • NAFLD/MASH: Weight loss most effective treatment (even 5-10% weight loss reduces liver fat and inflammation). Mediterranean-style diet evidence-based for NAFLD. Avoid fructose-containing drinks (high-fructose corn syrup, soft drinks). Regular aerobic exercise.

Micronutrient Considerations in Cirrhosis

  • Zinc deficiency: common in cirrhosis. Zinc supplementation may improve hepatic encephalopathy and immune function.
  • Thiamine (Vitamin B1): deficiency in alcohol use disorder. Parenteral thiamine before IV dextrose to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): malabsorbed in cholestatic liver disease. Monitor and supplement.

IBD Nutrition

Active Disease

  • Enteral nutrition (liquid formula feeds): as effective as steroids for induction of remission in paediatric Crohn's disease. Less effective in adults for inducing remission but used to support nutrition and bowel rest.
  • Elemental/polymeric feeds: absorbed in the small bowel, reduce antigenic load. Used for Crohn's disease flares, post-surgical periods, growth failure in children.
  • Low-residue diet: during small bowel obstruction or strictures in Crohn's — avoid high-fibre foods, nuts, seeds, raw vegetables. Soft-cooked well-chewed foods.

Nutritional Deficiencies in IBD

  • Iron deficiency anaemia: very common. IV iron preferred over oral in active IBD (oral iron not well absorbed and may worsen intestinal inflammation). Monitor regularly.
  • Vitamin B12: ileal Crohn's disease or terminal ileal resection impairs B12 absorption (intrinsic factor-mediated absorption in terminal ileum). IM B12 injections (hydroxocobalamin) 3-monthly if ileum affected/resected.
  • Vitamin D: almost universally low. Supplement D3 1000-2000 IU/day. Monitor 25-OH Vitamin D.
  • Folate: mesalazine impairs folate absorption. 5mg folic acid daily recommended on mesalazine. Essential if methotrexate used.
  • Calcium: corticosteroid use + malabsorption + dietary avoidance of dairy increase osteoporosis risk. Supplement calcium 1000mg/day + Vitamin D.
Hepatology

Liver Health

Comprehensive research on liver diseases, cirrhosis complications, and liver health from EASL and BSG guidelines.

Cirrhosis and Its Complications

Cirrhosis is the irreversible end-stage of liver fibrosis, characterised by diffuse nodular regeneration with fibrous bands distorting hepatic architecture and portal hypertension. Child-Pugh and MELD scores quantify severity. All cirrhotics require HCC surveillance (6-monthly USS +/- AFP).

Complications of Cirrhosis

  • Ascites: Sodium restriction, spironolactone +/- furosemide, therapeutic paracentesis (with albumin 6-8g/L drained for volumes over 5L). TIPSS if refractory. Avoid NSAIDs.
  • SBP (Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis): Diagnostic tap if new ascites or deterioration. PMN over 250/mm3 = SBP. IV antibiotics (cefotaxime/tazobactam), IV albumin (reduces hepatorenal syndrome risk). Long-term norfloxacin prophylaxis after first episode.
  • Oesophageal varices: Gastroscopy to assess. Non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol/carvedilol) for primary prophylaxis (medium/large varices). Acute bleeding: terlipressin + endoscopic band ligation. TIPSS for refractory bleeding.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy (HE): Identify and treat precipitants (infection, GI bleeding, constipation, electrolyte disturbance, medications). Lactulose to 2-3 soft stools/day. Rifaximin 550mg BD for recurrent HE.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome (HRS): Functional renal failure in cirrhosis. Type 1 (rapidly progressive) and Type 2 (slower). Terlipressin + albumin for Type 1 HRS. Avoid nephrotoxics (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, IV contrast where possible).

NAFLD / MASLD / MASH

Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) / Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD): Fat accumulation in the liver in the absence of significant alcohol use, associated with metabolic risk factors (obesity, T2DM, dyslipidaemia, hypertension). Most common liver disease globally. Spectrum: simple steatosis (benign course in most) through NASH/MASH (active inflammation and injury) to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and HCC.

  • Assessment: LFTs, fibroscan (CAP score for steatosis, liver stiffness for fibrosis), FIB-4 score (age x AST / (platelets x sqrt ALT)). High FIB-4 (over 2.67) or high fibroscan stiffness: refer to hepatology. MRI-PDFF: most accurate fat quantification.
  • Management: Weight loss 5-10% improves steatosis; 10%+ can reverse NASH and fibrosis. Mediterranean diet recommended. Aerobic exercise 150 minutes/week. Treat metabolic risk factors (blood pressure, cholesterol, diabetes control). GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) reduce liver fat and NASH activity. Resmetirom (FDA 2024): first approved treatment for MASH with fibrosis (F2-F3).
GI Oncology

GI Cancer Screening & Surveillance

Evidence-based GI cancer screening, surveillance, and early detection from BSG, ACG and NICE guidelines.

Colorectal Cancer (CRC) Screening

  • FIT (Faecal Immunochemical Test): NHS programme: every 2 years, age 50-74 (being extended to age 45-50). Positive FIT (haemoglobin over 10 mcg/g): urgent colonoscopy referral. FIT is quantitative, patient-friendly, no dietary restrictions.
  • Average risk colonoscopy screening: Not standard in UK (used in USA). Single one-off flexible sigmoidoscopy at age 55 (NHS Bowel Scope programme) was piloted but not rolled out nationally.
  • High-risk groups: Personal history of CRC or adenomas (post-polypectomy surveillance), family history (first-degree relative under 60 with CRC: colonoscopy from age 35-40 or 10 years before relative's age of diagnosis). Hereditary syndromes: annual/biennial colonoscopy.
  • Lynch syndrome: Colonoscopy every 1-2 years from age 25. All CRC should have universal MMR immunohistochemistry testing.
  • FAP (Familial Adenomatous Polyposis): Annual flexible sigmoidoscopy from age 13-15. Colectomy usually needed by 20s (hundreds of polyps, near 100% lifetime CRC risk without surgery).

Barrett's Oesophagus Surveillance

  • No dysplasia: surveillance OGD every 3-5 years (BSG guidelines). Optimise PPI therapy.
  • Low-grade dysplasia (LGD) confirmed by two pathologists: endoscopic eradication therapy (RFA) recommended in NICE-approved centres. Alternatively, 6-monthly surveillance if patient declines therapy.
  • High-grade dysplasia (HGD) or T1a cancer: Endoscopic therapy (EMR for visible lesions + RFA for flat Barrett's). Highly curative if caught at this stage (over 95% cure with endotherapy vs major oesophagectomy surgery previously required).

H. pylori and Gastric Cancer

  • H. pylori is a WHO class 1 carcinogen for gastric cancer. Eradication reduces gastric cancer risk by approximately 35-40% (especially if eradicated before development of gastric atrophy).
  • Gastric cancer screening: No national programme in UK/India. High-risk individuals (family history of gastric cancer, confirmed H. pylori + atrophic gastritis, previous partial gastrectomy): endoscopy surveillance programme.
  • High-risk populations: East Asian (Japanese, Korean, Chinese) populations have significantly higher gastric cancer rates. Countries with national gastric cancer screening (Japan, Korea) using OGD or double-contrast barium study from age 40-50.
  • Correa cascade: H. pylori → chronic gastritis → gastric atrophy → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → gastric adenocarcinoma. Eradication before atrophy stage most protective.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) Surveillance

  • Who needs surveillance: All cirrhotic patients. HBV-infected patients without cirrhosis (certain subgroups per EASL criteria: Asian males over 40, Asian females over 50, African origin, family history HCC).
  • Surveillance protocol: 6-monthly abdominal ultrasound +/- AFP. CT or MRI liver if USS identifies lesion over 1cm or AFP rising.
  • LIRADS classification: LI-RADS 1-2 (benign), LI-RADS 3 (intermediate), LI-RADS 4 (probably HCC), LI-RADS 5 (definitely HCC = diagnostic for HCC without biopsy if typical imaging features on CT/MRI).
  • Treatment: BCLC staging guides treatment. Early (BCLC 0-A): surgical resection, ablation (RFA/MWA), or liver transplantation (Milan criteria). Intermediate (BCLC B): TACE (trans-arterial chemoembolisation). Advanced (BCLC C): sorafenib/lenvatinib or atezolizumab+bevacizumab (immunotherapy).
